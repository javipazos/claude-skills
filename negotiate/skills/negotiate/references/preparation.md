# Preparation Phase

Complete this phase before drafting any message. Written negotiation's greatest advantage is time — use it.

## 1. BATNA Assessment

**Source:** Fisher & Ury (1981), Harvard Negotiation Project. Most cited concept in negotiation literature.

The Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement determines walk-away power. Without a clear BATNA, the negotiation is blind.

### Diagnostic Questions

Ask the user:
- What happens if this negotiation fails completely? What's the realistic alternative?
- What's the other party's likely alternative if they don't reach a deal with you?
- Can you improve your BATNA before or during this negotiation?

### Application

- A strong BATNA means more leverage — can push harder, concede less
- A weak BATNA means focus on creating value, not claiming it
- Never reveal a weak BATNA. Never fabricate a strong one
- Reference alternatives subtly: "I'm evaluating several options and yours is compelling because..." (implies alternatives without threatening)
- Detect weak BATNA in the other party: excessive urgency, unprompted concessions, repeated follow-ups

## 2. Interest Mapping

**Source:** Fisher & Ury's second principle: focus on interests, not positions.

Positions are what people say they want. Interests are why they want it. Most negotiations have more room for creative solutions than the initial positions suggest.

### Diagnostic Questions

- What does the user actually need from this deal? (Not just what they're asking for)
- What might the other party need beyond what they've stated?
- Are there issues where one side cares deeply and the other doesn't? (Trade opportunities)
- Are there shared interests that both sides benefit from?

### Application

- Create a two-column map: User's interests vs Other party's likely interests
- Identify trades: things cheap for one side to give but valuable for the other
- In salary negotiations: title, flexibility, start date, signing bonus, review timeline, equity are all tradeable beyond base salary
- In vendor contracts: volume, payment terms, exclusivity, support level, contract length are tradeable beyond unit price

## 3. Anchoring Strategy

**Source:** Tversky & Kahneman (1974). One of the most replicated findings in behavioral science. Galinsky & Mussweiler (2001) confirmed first offers strongly influence final outcomes.

### When to Anchor First

Anchor first when:
- Good information about the range exists (market data, comparable deals)
- The other party has less information than you
- There's no established price or precedent

Let them anchor first when:
- Uncertain about the range and they may offer more than expected
- Their anchor will reveal information about their constraints
- Cultural norms expect the buyer/requester to state terms first

### Precise Numbers

Mason et al. (2013): precise, non-round numbers ($73,500 vs $70,000) are perceived as more informed and researched. In written negotiation, this effect is amplified because the number stays visible in the thread.

### Counter-Anchoring

When the other party anchors first:
- Explicitly acknowledge it as a starting point to reduce its power: "I appreciate the starting point. Let me share the data I'm working from..."
- Consider the opposite (Galinsky & Mussweiler): deliberately think about why that number is wrong before responding
- Do not split the difference from their anchor — that's still being anchored

## 4. Reference Point Analysis

**Source:** Kahneman & Tversky (1979), Prospect Theory. Nobel Prize in Economics (2002).

People evaluate outcomes relative to a reference point, not in absolute terms. Losses from the reference point hurt ~2x more than equivalent gains feel good.

### Diagnostic Questions

- What's the other party's current status quo? (This is their reference point)
- Will your proposal feel like a gain or a loss relative to their status quo?
- Can you shift the reference point before making the proposal?

### Application

- Frame proposals to minimize perceived loss from their reference point
- Present what they keep, not what they give up
- Frame requests as restoring a status quo rather than asking for something new: "Given the market has moved 12% since our last agreement, this adjustment brings us back in line" (restoration, not demand)
- Detect when loss framing is being used on you: "You'll miss out on...", "This offer expires...", "You're losing X every day you don't..."

## 5. Accusation Audit

**Source:** Voss, supported by inoculation theory (McGuire, 1964) and "steal thunder" effect (Williams et al., 1993).

List the worst things the other party could think about your position. Say them first. Preemptive disclosure of negatives reduces their impact compared to discovery by the other side.

### Application in Written Negotiation

Draft 2-3 sentences acknowledging likely objections before they're raised:

- "I realize this request comes at a busy time for your team..."
- "I know the budget has been tight this quarter, so I want to be upfront about what I'm asking and why..."
- "You might be thinking this timeline is aggressive — and you'd be right. Here's how I've thought about making it work..."

### Guidelines

- Be genuine, not performative — acknowledge real concerns, not straw men
- Place near the beginning of the message, before the ask
- Don't overdo it — 2-3 acknowledgments maximum. More reads as insecure
- In ongoing relationships, this builds trust by showing awareness of the other side's position
