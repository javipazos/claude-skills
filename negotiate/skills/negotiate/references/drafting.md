# Drafting Phase

Apply these techniques when composing the negotiation message. Each technique includes its evidence base and specific guidance for written application.

## 1. Affect Labeling

**Source:** Voss's "labeling" technique. Evidence: Lieberman et al. (2007, UCLA, fMRI study): putting feelings into words reduces amygdala activation. Meta-analysis by Costafreda et al. (2008) confirmed the effect.

Name the other party's likely emotions or concerns. In writing, labeling is arguably more precise than verbal — there is time to choose the exact right word.

### Written Patterns

- "It seems like timeline is your main concern here..."
- "I can see how this feels like a significant change from what we discussed..."
- "It sounds like predictability matters more than the absolute number..."

### Guidelines

- Use tentative language ("it seems", "it sounds like") — asserting someone's feelings reads as presumptuous
- Label the concern behind the position, not the position itself
- Labeling works in both directions: label their concerns to reduce defensiveness, label your own to build transparency
- In email, labeling compensates for the medium's emotion-reading deficit (research shows only ~22% agreement on interpreting emotions in email)

## 2. Loss Aversion and Framing

**Source:** Kahneman & Tversky (1979), Prospect Theory. People fear losses ~2x more than they value equivalent gains.

### Framing Principles for Drafting

- **Frame as loss prevention, not gain:** "If we don't finalize by Friday, we lose the early pricing" is stronger than "If we finalize by Friday, we get a discount"
- **Frame concessions from their reference point:** Present what they keep, not what they give up
- **Aggregate losses, separate gains:** If giving multiple concessions, present them as one package. If offering multiple benefits, list them separately
- **Frame requests as restoration:** "This adjustment brings us in line with market rates" (restoring fairness) rather than "I want more money" (demanding a gain)

### Detecting Loss Framing Used on You

Flag these patterns in messages the user receives:
- "You'll miss out on..." / "This offer expires..."
- "You're losing X every day you don't..."
- "Other candidates are ready to move forward..."
- Artificial deadlines without clear business justification

When detected, advise: acknowledge the framing, reframe in neutral terms, and evaluate the substance independently of the urgency.

## 3. Calibrated Questions

**Source:** Voss. Consistent with Self-Determination Theory (Deci & Ryan, 1985/2000): autonomy drives cooperation. Also related to IKEA effect (Norton et al., 2012): people value what they helped create.

"How" and "What" questions that give the other party a sense of control while shifting the problem-solving burden to them.

### Written Patterns

Instead of rejecting → ask how:
- ~~"I can't accept that price"~~ → "How can we close the gap between our numbers?"
- ~~"That deadline doesn't work"~~ → "What would help make a later timeline feasible on your end?"
- ~~"That's not enough"~~ → "How would you suggest we address the market data I'm seeing?"

### Why They Work in Writing

- Cannot be answered with yes/no — require thoughtful response
- In text, they read as collaborative rather than challenging
- Buy time legitimately — asking a question is a natural way to slow down without seeming evasive
- Create a written record of the other party proposing solutions (commitment/consistency)

### Guidelines

- Use "how" and "what", avoid "why" (feels accusatory in writing)
- Limit to 1-2 calibrated questions per message — more feels like an interrogation
- Follow a calibrated question with a concrete proposal or data point — don't leave it hanging

## 4. Cialdini's Influence Principles (Written Application)

**Source:** Cialdini (2009). Tested across 642 participants by Mollazehi et al. (2024). Reciprocity showed largest effect (Cohen's d = 1.05).

### Reciprocity (Strongest in written context)

Offer value before asking. In email, this means:
- Share useful information, research, or analysis before making your request
- Make a concession first (even a small one) to create obligation
- "I've done some research on this and wanted to share what I found before we discuss numbers..."

### Commitment/Consistency (Very strong in written context)

Written commitments are stronger than verbal ones. Leverage what the other party has already written:
- "In your email on [date], you mentioned that long-term partnerships are important to your team..."
- "As we agreed in our last exchange, the key criteria are..."
- Reference their stated values, priorities, or principles — people act consistently with what they've written

### Social Proof (Moderate)

Reference what others do, but tie it to specific data:
- "Industry standard for this role in [city] is..." (with source)
- "Comparable properties in this area have been renting for..."
- Avoid vague social proof ("everyone does it") — in writing, vague claims are easy to dismiss

### Authority (Strong in writing)

Reference credible sources, data, and expert opinions:
- "According to [specific source]..."
- "The market data from [platform] shows..."
- Authority in writing comes from specificity and sources, not from asserting expertise

### Liking (Lower in email — compensate deliberately)

Harder to build rapport in text. Compensate by:
- Matching their communication style (formal/casual, brief/detailed)
- Using their terminology (if they say "investment" don't say "cost")
- Acknowledging their constraints genuinely
- Opening with something specific to them, not a generic greeting

### When Principles Conflict

- In ongoing relationships, prioritize reciprocity and commitment/consistency
- In one-shot negotiations, authority and social proof carry more weight
- If you detect the other party using these on you, name the principle internally and evaluate substance separately

## 5. Language and Style Adaptation

**Source:** Chameleon effect adapted from Chartrand & Bargh (1999). Physical mimicry builds rapport; in text, language adaptation serves the same function.

- Mirror their terminology — use their words for key concepts
- Match formality level — if they write formally, respond formally
- Match structure — if they use bullet points, respond with bullet points
- Match length — a three-paragraph email doesn't warrant a one-line response, and vice versa
- Echo key phrases to demonstrate careful reading

## 6. Proactive Medium Management

**Source:** Parlamis & Geiger: negotiators who proactively managed the email medium achieved more joint gain.

In written negotiation, explicitly manage expectations about the medium itself:
- Set response time expectations: "I'll review this carefully and get back to you by Thursday"
- Suggest format changes when helpful: "This might be easier to resolve in a quick call — would 15 minutes work?"
- Rephrase unclear points from their message to confirm understanding
- Number your proposals/points for easy reference in replies

### Why This Matters

Research shows email negotiations have a ~50% stalemate rate (vs 19% face-to-face). Proactive medium management directly reduces this by preventing misunderstandings and maintaining momentum.
