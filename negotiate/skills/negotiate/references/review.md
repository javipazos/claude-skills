# Review Phase — Pre-Send Checklist

Before sending any negotiation message, verify each point. In written negotiation, every word stays on the record — there is no taking it back.

## Tone Calibration

- [ ] **Is the tone calmer than your emotional state?** Written words read harsher than intended. Kruger et al. found people consistently overestimate how accurately recipients interpret tone in email. When in doubt, soften.
- [ ] **Remove exclamation marks in substantive sections.** They undermine authority in negotiation context. Reserve for genuinely warm openings/closings if appropriate.
- [ ] **Read the message imagining you're the recipient having a bad day.** If any sentence could be read as aggressive, hostile, or dismissive under that lens, rewrite it.
- [ ] **Explicit emotion signals present?** Because natural emotion reading fails in text (~22% agreement rate among trained coders), successful email negotiators explicitly label their own state: "I'm genuinely excited about this possibility" or "I want to be candid about my concern here."

## Concession Check

- [ ] **Are you giving anything without getting something?** Every concession should be paired with a request or at least acknowledged as a deliberate goodwill gesture.
- [ ] **Are your concessions framed as costly?** Even easy concessions should be presented as meaningful: "I've thought about this carefully and I'm willing to..." (not "Sure, no problem").
- [ ] **Concession pattern check:** If this is a multi-round negotiation, are your concessions decreasing in size? Consistent or increasing concessions signal that there's more to give.

## Anchor Awareness

- [ ] **Have you been unconsciously anchored?** If responding to their proposal, did you start your thinking from their number and adjust, or did you independently calculate your target first?
- [ ] **Counter-anchor present?** If their message contained a number, does your response include your own anchor with supporting rationale?
- [ ] **Precise numbers used?** Non-round numbers ($73,500 vs $75,000) signal research and preparation.

## Framing Check

- [ ] **Loss frame applied?** Is your proposal framed in terms of what they gain or keep, not what you need?
- [ ] **Reference point considered?** Does the message account for their likely status quo?
- [ ] **Restoration vs demand?** Where possible, frame requests as restoring fairness, alignment with market, or consistency with prior agreements.

## Structure and Clarity

- [ ] **One core ask per message?** Bundling too many arguments creates information overload and increases contentiousness. If multiple points are necessary, prioritize and number them.
- [ ] **Calibrated questions present?** At least one "how" or "what" question that invites collaboration rather than forcing a yes/no.
- [ ] **Proactive medium management?** Response timeline set, format clarified, next steps explicit.
- [ ] **Key points scannable?** In email, the recipient may skim. Critical points should be impossible to miss — use structure (bold, bullets, short paragraphs).

## Relationship Check

- [ ] **Appropriate for the relationship type?** Aggressive tactics in an ongoing relationship (colleague, long-term vendor, landlord you'll see for years) cause damage that outweighs short-term gains.
- [ ] **Bridges intact?** Even if this round doesn't close, the door should remain open. Avoid ultimatums unless genuinely prepared to walk away.
- [ ] **Scarcity/urgency justified?** If applying time pressure, is it based on real constraints or manufactured urgency? The latter destroys trust when detected.

## Final Pass

- [ ] **Read it once more as the recipient.** What will they think? What will they feel? What will they do?
- [ ] **Is there anything you'll regret if this gets forwarded?** Email gets forwarded. Write accordingly.
- [ ] **Wait before sending.** If the negotiation is high-stakes, draft now, review in 1-2 hours or tomorrow. The asynchronous advantage is your greatest asset — use it.
