# Anti-Patterns in Written Negotiation

Behaviors to actively flag and warn against. Each is backed by research on what fails specifically in asynchronous/written negotiation.

## 1. Splitting the Difference

**Why it fails:** Feels fair but is mathematically arbitrary. If Party A asks 100 and Party B asks 60, "meeting in the middle" at 80 rewards whoever made the more extreme opening. It also eliminates creative solutions — there may be a deal at 70 + a non-monetary term that's worth more to both sides.

**Instead:** Explore interests behind the numbers. Use calibrated questions: "How can we structure this so it works for both of us?"

## 2. Bundling Too Many Arguments

**Source:** Parlamis & Geiger research on email negotiation behaviors.

**Why it fails:** Information overload in writing increases contentiousness. The recipient picks the weakest argument to attack and ignores the rest. More arguments ≠ more persuasive.

**Instead:** Lead with the strongest 1-2 points. Hold remaining arguments for subsequent rounds if needed. Number points for easy reference.

## 3. Assuming Tone Will Be Read as Intended

**Source:** Kruger et al. (NYU/Chicago): people consistently overestimate tone accuracy in email. Laubert & Parlamis: trained coders agreed on email emotions only ~22% of the time.

**Why it fails:** Sarcasm reads as hostility. Humor reads as dismissiveness. Brevity reads as anger. The gap between intended and perceived tone is massive in text.

**Instead:** Be more explicit about emotional state than feels necessary. "I want to be clear this isn't frustration — I'm genuinely trying to find a path forward." Over-signal warmth and good faith.

## 4. Scarcity Pressure in Ongoing Relationships

**Source:** Cialdini's research shows scarcity has the smallest effect size of all influence principles. Mollazehi et al. (2024) confirmed this.

**Why it fails:** "This offer expires Friday" works in one-shot sales. In ongoing relationships (employer, long-term vendor, landlord), it signals distrust and creates resentment. Even if it works once, it damages future negotiations.

**Instead:** Use commitment/consistency ("As you mentioned, you value retention...") or reciprocity ("I've been flexible on X, and I'd appreciate flexibility on Y").

## 5. Humor in High-Stakes Written Negotiation

**Source:** Research shows humor can increase trust in email but is frequently misunderstood.

**Why it fails:** Without vocal tone and facial expression, humor is ambiguous. A joke about the other party's position can read as mockery. Self-deprecating humor can read as weakness. The risk/reward ratio in written negotiation is poor.

**Instead:** Be warm and human without attempting humor. Genuine acknowledgment of the situation ("I know these conversations are never easy") builds more rapport than jokes.

## 6. Reactive Medium Management

**Source:** Parlamis & Geiger: reactive medium management (addressing problems after they occur) actually decreased joint gain in email negotiations.

**Why it fails:** Fixing a misunderstanding after it happens is harder than preventing it. "I think there was a miscommunication" signals that the process is already off track.

**Instead:** Proactively set expectations: response timelines, how to handle disagreements, numbering proposals for reference, suggesting calls for complex points.

## 7. Relationship-Building Chitchat in One-Shot Negotiations

**Source:** Research on email negotiation found that in one-shot contexts, relationship-building behaviors did not improve outcomes or satisfaction.

**Why it fails:** In a one-shot email negotiation (buying a car, negotiating a freelance rate with someone you won't work with again), small talk wastes space and can delay getting to substance.

**Instead:** Be polite and professional, but focus energy on value-creating moves: multi-issue offers, suggesting compromises, identifying shared interests.

## 8. Responding Immediately to Provocative Messages

**Why it fails:** Email's asynchronous nature is an advantage — it prevents impulsive responses. Responding in anger produces messages that escalate conflict and weaken your position.

**Instead:** Draft a response immediately (it helps process the emotion), then wait minimum 2 hours before reviewing. The message you send should be strategic, not reactive.

## 9. Making Unilateral Concessions Under Pressure

**Why it fails:** Unpaired concessions signal weakness and invite more pressure. In written negotiation, the concession is on permanent record and becomes the new baseline.

**Instead:** Every concession should be explicitly paired: "I can be flexible on timeline if we can align on price." If making a goodwill concession, frame it as costly and deliberate, not as capitulation.

## 10. Ignoring the Written Record

**Why it fails:** In email negotiation, everything is documented. Contradicting your own prior statements destroys credibility. Failing to reference the other party's prior commitments wastes leverage.

**Instead:** Before drafting, re-read the full thread. Reference specific prior statements (yours and theirs). Build arguments on the established record.
