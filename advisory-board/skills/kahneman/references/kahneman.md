# Daniel Kahneman — Frameworks and Teachings

## System 1 / System 2 (Dual-Process Theory)
**Source:** *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (2011), Part I (Chapters 1–9); Nobel Lecture "Maps of Bounded Rationality" (2002)

**System 1:** Fast, automatic, emotional, associative, always on. Handles face recognition, reading simple sentences, intuitive pattern-matching.

**System 2:** Slow, effortful, deliberate, conscious. Handles complex calculations, monitoring social behavior, parking in tight spaces.

System 1 continuously generates suggestions — impressions, intuitions, impulses. System 2 typically endorses them without auditing. The critical failure: System 2 is lazy and accepts System 1's outputs as true.

**When to trust intuition:** In stable environments with frequent feedback (expert chess players, experienced firefighters), intuition is calibrated expertise. In irregular, low-feedback environments (stock markets, clinical prognosis), it's a System 1 shortcut requiring System 2 scrutiny.

**Decision lens:** Before acting on a gut feeling, ask: Is this a domain where I've received reliable, repeated feedback? If not, slow down deliberately.

## The Three Core Heuristics
**Source:** Tversky & Kahneman, "Judgment under Uncertainty: Heuristics and Biases" (*Science*, 1974); *TFS* Chapters 11-14

**1. Representativeness (Chapter 14):**
People judge probability by how closely something resembles a stereotype. Produces base rate neglect and the conjunction fallacy (Linda Problem: 85% of subjects said "bank teller AND feminist" was more probable than "bank teller" alone — a logical impossibility).

**Counter:** Before judging probability from a description, ask what the base rate is. Weight it heavily.

**2. Availability (Chapter 12):**
People estimate frequency by ease of recall. Plane crashes feel more probable after media coverage. Words starting with K are easier to generate than words with K in third position, so people misjudge the ratio.

**Counter:** Ask "Is this easy to recall because it's common, or because it's dramatic?" Seek statistical data.

**3. Anchoring (Chapter 11):**
People start from an initial value and adjust insufficiently. Subjects anchored at 1,200 feet estimated tallest redwood at 844 feet; anchored at 180 feet, they estimated 282 feet. Same question, opposite anchors, radically different answers.

**Counter:** Generate your own estimate before receiving any external number. Actively consider values far outside the anchor range.

## Prospect Theory
**Source:** Kahneman & Tversky, "Prospect Theory: An Analysis of Decision under Risk" (*Econometrica*, 1979); *TFS* Part IV (Chapters 26–34)

Nobel Prize-winning theory of how people actually decide under risk, replacing Expected Utility Theory.

**Three core principles:**

**1. Reference Dependence:** People evaluate outcomes as gains and losses relative to a reference point (usually status quo), not as absolute states. The same outcome can feel like a gain or loss depending on expectations.

**2. Loss Aversion:** Losses loom approximately twice as large as equivalent gains. The value function is steeper in the loss domain. This explains why people simultaneously buy insurance and lottery tickets.

**3. Diminishing Sensitivity:** The difference between $10 and $20 feels larger than between $1,010 and $1,020. Concave for gains (risk aversion), convex for losses (risk seeking).

**The Endowment Effect (Chapter 27):** People demand more to give up an object than they'd pay to acquire it — sellers demanded ~2x what buyers offered for identical coffee mugs.

**Decision lens:**
- Reframe decisions in terms of expected outcomes rather than gains/losses from an arbitrary reference point
- Before rejecting a risky option, ask: Am I avoiding because it's genuinely risky, or because I'm loss-averse?
- In negotiations: structure proposals to feel like preventing losses rather than achieving gains

## Overconfidence: Illusion of Validity and the Outside View
**Source:** *TFS* Part III (Chapters 19–24)

**Illusion of Validity (Chapter 20):** Kahneman's team made confident predictions about Israeli military candidates. Predictions showed no correlation with actual performance. Knowing this didn't change their confidence next time. "The confidence felt strong and entirely appropriate... even when we knew the conclusions were unreliable."

**Illusion of Understanding (Chapter 19):** "The illusion that we understand the past fosters overconfidence in our ability to predict the future." Hindsight bias makes past events feel inevitable.

**Planning Fallacy (Chapter 23):** Systematic tendency to underestimate time, costs, and risks while overestimating benefits. Kahneman's curriculum project: team estimated 18-30 months. Similar projects took 7-10 years. Actual time: 8 years.

**Reference Class Forecasting:** Identify comparable past projects and use their actual outcomes as your baseline before consulting your specific case.

**Tools:**
- **Premortem:** Before committing, assume the project has failed. Write a brief account of why. Activates knowledge System 1 suppresses under optimism.
- **Outside view:** Step outside your situation and look at it as a statistical outsider with access to the record of similar attempts.

## WYSIATI and Cognitive Ease
**Source:** *TFS* Chapters 5–7

**WYSIATI — What You See Is All There Is:**
System 1 constructs the best possible story from available information without flagging what's absent. "We often fail to allow for the possibility that evidence that should be critical to our judgment is missing."

This explains why:
- Confident people are trusted more (confidence is a story cue, not accuracy cue)
- Framing effects are powerful ("1 in 1,000 will die" vs. "99.9% survival rate")
- Less information can produce more confidence (cleaner narrative)

**Cognitive Ease:** System 1 uses fluency as a proxy for truth. Clear fonts, repeated ideas, rhyming statements all feel more true. This is not minor — it's the substrate of propaganda and unfounded confidence.

**Counter:** Before forming a strong opinion, ask: What am I not seeing? What would a well-informed skeptic know? What would have to be true for me to be wrong?

## Experiencing Self vs. Remembering Self
**Source:** TED Talk "The Riddle of Experience vs. Memory" (2010); *TFS* Part V (Chapters 35–38)

**Experiencing self:** lives in present-moment reality ("does it hurt right now?")
**Remembering self:** maintains the life story ("how was it overall?")

These conflict systematically. The remembering self ignores duration almost entirely.

**Peak-End Rule:** Memory is dominated by the most intense moment (peak) and how it ended. Duration neglect: how long it lasted contributes almost nothing to how it's remembered.

**Colonoscopy study:** Patient B had a longer procedure but gentler ending. Reported better memory despite more total pain.

**Focusing Illusion (Chapter 38):** "Nothing in life is as important as you think it is when you are thinking about it." We inflate the contribution of whatever we're attending to.

**Decision lens:**
- Engineer endings — they disproportionately shape memory
- Distinguish between optimizing for experience (the moment) vs. memory (the story)
- Before a major life change, ask: Will this improve daily experiential state, or just the story?

## Noise: The Underdiagnosed Error
**Source:** *Noise: A Flaw in Human Judgment* (2021, with Sibony and Sunstein)

**Bias** = average of error (systematic, directional). **Noise** = variability of error (unpredictable scatter).

MSE = Bias² + Noise². Noise contributes equally to total error but gets almost no attention.

**Three types of noise:**
- **Level Noise:** Judges differ in average severity
- **Pattern Noise:** Individuals weight case features differently
- **Occasion Noise:** Same person, different judgment at different times (fatigue, mood, order of cases)

**Sentencing study:** 208 federal judges, identical cases. Average sentence: 7 years. Average difference between any two judges: 4 years.

**Decision Hygiene (practical framework):**
1. Conduct a noise audit — same cases to multiple decision-makers, measure variance
2. Sequence information deliberately — avoid contaminating early impressions
3. Delay intuitive judgment — independent assessments before group discussion
4. Use independent aggregation — average multiple independent judgments
5. Use structure — checklists and scoring rubrics reduce occasion noise

**Decision lens:** Before assuming a poor decision is bias, run a noise audit. If three different people with the same information would produce very different outputs, noise is your primary problem — and it's addressed through process design, not awareness training.
