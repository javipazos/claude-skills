# Rory Sutherland — Frameworks and Teachings

## Psycho-Logic vs. Logic
**Source:** *Alchemy* (2019), Chapter 1; Edge.org conversation (2014)

"The human mind does not run on logic any more than a horse runs on petrol."

Logic is the language of engineering; psycho-logic is how humans actually decide. Economics has been catastrophically over-reliant on logical models.

"Any species that's rational also becomes highly predictable, and anybody who's highly predictable is rapidly dead." Pure rationality was never selected for in evolution.

**Before proposing a rational solution, ask:** Is this a logic problem or a psycho-logic problem? Above basic needs, the bottleneck is usually perception, not reality.

## The Psychological Moonshot
**Source:** *Alchemy* (2019); TED Talk "Perspective is Everything" (2011)

A small-cost intervention in perception that achieves disproportionate improvement in satisfaction — without changing reality.

**Key examples:**
- Eurostar spent £6 billion to reduce London-Paris travel by 40 minutes. A fraction spent on WiFi and free champagne would have improved satisfaction more.
- Uber's map doesn't make the taxi arrive faster — it makes waiting 90% less frustrating
- London Underground countdown boards reduced frustration dramatically without adding a single train

"It's easy to achieve massive improvements in perception at a fraction of the cost of equivalent improvements in reality."

**Before spending on a costly improvement, ask:** Is there a psychological intervention — information, framing, context, sequence — that achieves an equivalent outcome?

## The Four Drivers of Psycho-Logic
**Source:** *Alchemy* (2019), Chapters 3–5

**1. Signalling:** Costly signals are credible signals. An expensive engagement ring communicates commitment precisely because it's expensive. "Reciprocation, reputation and pre-commitment signalling are the three big mechanisms that underpin trust."

**2. Self-Signalling:** People signal to themselves. Luxury purchases, rituals, placebos function as messages we send ourselves. "We spend considerable time and money essentially signaling to ourselves."

**3. Satisficing:** People don't maximize; they look for the "least worst" option. Brand preference is rational: "I will pay a premium as insurance for the reduced likelihood that this product is appalling." McDonald's: not the best burger, but you won't get food poisoning.

**4. Psychophysics:** Value is never absolute; it's relative to a reference point. Same wine tastes better when told it costs more. Context is part of the product.

## The Arithmocracy Problem
**Source:** *Alchemy* (2019); The Spectator column

"Arithmocracy" (term by Anthony Tasgal): the class of quantitative decision-makers whose worship of measurement makes them incapable of imagining unmeasurable improvements.

"It is much easier to be fired for being illogical than for being unimaginative."

**The Doorman Fallacy:** Consultants defined a hotel doorman's role as "opening the door" and recommended an automatic mechanism. This destroyed unmeasurable value — status signal, human presence, early-warning system.

**Before any decision, ask:** What value would be invisible on a spreadsheet? Is this decision being made because it's actually best, or because it's defensible?

## Reframing as Problem-Solving
**Source:** TED Talks "Perspective is Everything" (2011) and "Life Lessons from an Ad Man" (2009); *Alchemy* section 6.8

"A change in perceived value can be just as satisfying as what we consider 'real' value."

**Key examples:**
- Frederick the Great's potato strategy: declared potatoes royal vegetables, planted behind guarded fences, let peasants steal them. Rebranded through social proof and scarcity.
- Antibiotic compliance: 18 white pills + 6 blue pills with instructions to take white first, then blue, increased completion rates vs. 24 identical pills. Milestone of finishing white created psychological momentum.
- Charitable toll road: motorists resent paying for premium lanes; reframe as charitable donation and moral calculus inverts.

"The opposite of a good idea can also be a good idea."

**Before changing reality, ask:** Can the frame be altered? Can the reference point be shifted? Can the question be changed so the desired answer becomes obvious?

## Loss Aversion and Variance Reduction
**Source:** *Alchemy* (2019); Edge.org (2014)

"The pain of losing £100 is twice as great as the joy of gaining £100, because in the wild, loss often meant death."

People minimize probability of catastrophic outcomes, not maximize expected value. Brand premiums reduce variance — you know you won't get something appalling.

Thaler's "Save More Tomorrow" pension plan: contributions deducted only from future pay rises. No experienced reduction in take-home pay. Pension contributions 200% higher than normal.

**When trying to change behavior, ask:** How do I remove the loss the person is trying to avoid? Loss framing ("don't miss this") typically outperforms gain framing ("take advantage of this").

## Context as Product
**Source:** *Alchemy* (2019); "Perspective is Everything" TED Talk (2011)

Value is never intrinsic — it's object plus context.

"A floor sweeper creates as much value as a chef, if the chef is in a restaurant with a filthy floor."

**Key examples:**
- Red Bull: more expensive, smaller, worse-tasting than competitors. Every attribute signals potency — taste says "medicine-grade, not candy"
- Playing French music in a wine shop increased French wine sales; German music increased German sales. Same products, different context, different behavior.

**Before changing a product, change its context.** What environment is it experienced in? Does that context amplify or suppress the value already present?

## The Bee Principle
**Source:** *Alchemy* (2019)

Bee colonies maintain "rogue" bees exploring suboptimal territory. Without this apparent waste, the colony can't discover better sources when current ones disappear.

Organizations that optimize ruthlessly for known metrics eliminate their capacity for breakthrough innovation.

"Remember, if you never do anything differently, you'll reduce your chances of lucky accidents."
"It doesn't pay to be logical if everyone else is being logical."

**Maintain intentional slack** for exploring what appears irrational. Ask: would rational competitors refuse to fund this? If yes, it deserves serious consideration.

## The Problem with Stated Preferences
**Source:** *Alchemy* (2019)

"The trouble with market research is people don't think what they feel, they don't say what they think, and they don't do what they say."

People post-rationalize decisions. Reasons given for choices are narratives constructed after the fact. Asking "would you buy this?" is unreliable.

**Better research question:** What behavior are people currently exhibiting, and what does that reveal about what they're actually trying to achieve? Observation of behavior trumps stated preference. Anomalies (things that "shouldn't" work but do) are the highest-value signal.

## The 11 Rules of Alchemy
1. The opposite of a good idea can also be a good idea
2. Don't design for average
3. It doesn't pay to be logical if everyone else is being logical
4. The nature of our attention affects the nature of our experience
5. A flower is simply a weed with an advertising budget
6. The problem with logic is that it kills off magic
7. A good guess which stands up to observation is still science
8. Test counterintuitive things only because no one else will
9. Solving problems using rationality is like playing golf with only one club
10. Dare to be trivial
11. If there were a logical answer, we would have found it already
