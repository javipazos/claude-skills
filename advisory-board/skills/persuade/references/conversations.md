# Frameworks Applied to Conversations and Interpersonal Influence

Checklist for applying the teachings when having difficult conversations, negotiating, persuading, or trying to be heard.

## Before the Conversation

- **Attunement** (Pink): Model the other person's perspective before you speak. What are their concerns, fears, constraints? What do they want to protect? What do they want to achieve? Write it down if it helps.
- **Reference point identification** (Kahneman): What is their current reference point? Any change from it will feel like a loss. Plan to acknowledge the reference point before proposing a shift.
- **Reframing preparation** (Sutherland): Is there a way to reframe the question so the desired outcome becomes the obvious answer? Prepare 2-3 alternative frames.
- **WHY clarity** (Sinek): Be clear on your own WHY before the conversation. What do you believe, and why does this matter beyond the immediate ask?

## Opening the Conversation

- **Circle of Safety first** (Sinek): Reduce threat signals. Start by establishing shared ground, shared concern, or shared identity. The other person needs to feel safe before they can hear you.
- **Interrogative approach** (Pink): Use questions over statements. "What would you think about...?" invites participation. "We should do X" invites resistance.
- **Start with WHY, not WHAT**: Don't open with the request. Open with the belief or concern that motivates the request. "I care about the long-term health of this club" before "I think we should change how we do X."

## During the Conversation

- **Psycho-logic over logic** (Sutherland): If your logical argument isn't landing, the problem may not be the logic — it may be the frame. Shift the frame instead of repeating the argument louder.
- **Loss aversion awareness** (Kahneman): If the other person is resisting, identify what they perceive as a loss. Acknowledge it explicitly. "I understand that changing this feels like giving up something that's worked." Then reframe the change as protecting against a larger loss.
- **Satisficing** (Sutherland): The other person may not need the optimal solution — they need confidence that your proposal is "definitely not terrible." Provide safety signals: precedent, reversibility, low risk of catastrophic failure.
- **Avoid anchoring traps** (Kahneman): If they introduce a number or position first, recognize it as an anchor. Don't adjust from it — generate your own independent assessment before responding.
- **WYSIATI check** (Kahneman): What information might be missing from either perspective? Ask: "What am I not seeing here?" or "What concerns do you have that I haven't addressed?"

## When It Gets Difficult

- **Buoyancy** (Pink): Stay afloat through resistance. Avoid the Three Ps — don't take pushback as Personal, Pervasive, or Permanent. It's specific, temporary, and about the situation.
- **Manipulation vs. Inspiration** (Sinek): If you catch yourself using fear, pressure, or urgency to force a decision, stop. That produces a transaction, not buy-in. Return to shared belief.
- **The focusing illusion** (Kahneman): "Nothing in life is as important as you think it is when you are thinking about it." If emotions are running high, both of you are overweighting the current moment. Acknowledge this openly.
- **Reframe, don't repeat** (Sutherland): If the same argument is getting the same resistance, the problem is the frame, not the volume. Try a completely different angle.

## Closing and After

- **Peak-End Rule** (Kahneman): How the conversation ends matters more than most of what happened during it. End on a positive, collaborative note — even if the middle was hard.
- **Connection regret test** (Pink): If you're hesitating to have a conversation at all, apply the regret test. At 80, will you regret not reaching out? The threshold for acting is almost always lower than you fear.
- **Boldness regret test** (Pink): If the conversation involves proposing something ambitious, same test. People who take bold action and fail adapt. People who don't act are left with permanent uncertainty.
- **Two diagnostic questions** (Pink): After any persuasion attempt, check: Did their situation genuinely improve? Is the world in a slightly better place? If yes, you persuaded ethically. If no, reconsider.

## Specific Scenarios

### With a Partner or Close Friend
- **Circle of Safety is paramount**: This is the relationship where safety matters most. Never use information shared in vulnerability as leverage.
- **Experiencing self vs. remembering self** (Kahneman): In conflict, the experiencing self feels pain now, but the remembering self will only retain the peak and the ending. Invest heavily in how the conversation ends.
- **Purpose over position** (Sinek): What's the shared WHY of the relationship? Return to it when positions diverge.

### In Club/Organization Settings
- **Infinite Game mindset** (Sinek): You're not trying to win this meeting; you're trying to keep the organization healthy long-term.
- **Worthy Rivals** (Sinek): Treat disagreement as learning, not competition. "What do they see that I don't?"
- **Psychological moonshots** (Sutherland): Small perceptual changes can shift group dynamics more than big structural changes. A renamed meeting, a different seating arrangement, a new ritual.

### Commercial Negotiations
- **Costly signalling** (Sutherland): Demonstrate investment through preparation, specificity, and personalization. Generic proposals signal low commitment.
- **Caveat venditor** (Pink): Frame yourself as serving their need, not closing a deal.
- **Anchoring strategy** (Kahneman): Present the ambitious option first. Let them adjust downward, not you adjust upward.
- **Variance reduction** (Sutherland): Reduce their perceived risk. Guarantees, pilot programs, references all serve as "insurance against appalling outcomes."
