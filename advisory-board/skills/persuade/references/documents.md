# Frameworks Applied to Documents and Presentations

Checklist for applying the teachings when creating proposals, presentations, pitch decks, and written arguments.

## Structure (Sinek + Pink)

- **Golden Circle order**: Open with WHY (the belief or problem), then HOW (your approach), then WHAT (the specifics). Never lead with features or specifications.
- **Pixar Pitch**: Structure the narrative arc — "Once upon a time... Every day... One day... Because of that... Until finally..." Forces causal storytelling with stakes and resolution.
- **Just Cause framing** (Sinek): Position the proposal as advancing a larger cause, not just completing a transaction. "We're not just building X, we're enabling Y."
- **Question Pitch** (Pink): When your underlying argument is strong, frame key points as questions. "What would it mean for the club to have a permanent home?" pulls the audience into participation.

## Persuasion Mechanics (Sutherland + Kahneman)

- **Reframe the problem first**: Before presenting the solution, reframe how the audience sees the problem. If they see it differently, they'll hear the solution differently.
- **Loss aversion**: Frame what the audience loses by NOT acting. "Without this, we risk X" is more powerful than "With this, we gain Y."
- **Anchoring**: Present the ambitious option first. Everything after feels reasonable by comparison.
- **Reference class**: When making projections, cite comparable cases (Kahneman's outside view). "Organizations similar to ours who did X saw Y results" beats unsupported claims.
- **Costly signals** (Sutherland): Include details that show you've invested effort — specific research, custom analysis, personalized data. Generic proposals signal low commitment.
- **Reduce noise**: Use structured arguments with consistent formatting. Inconsistency in a document triggers System 2 suspicion.

## Addressing Resistance (Kahneman + Sutherland)

- **WYSIATI counter**: Anticipate what the audience doesn't know they don't know. Surface the missing information before they discover it on their own.
- **Premortem framing**: Include a "risks and mitigations" section proactively. This activates knowledge that optimism suppresses and builds trust through transparency.
- **Satisficing**: Your audience may not read the whole document. Design for the satisficer — make the first page, the headings, and the conclusion each standalone-compelling.
- **Endowment effect**: If the audience already has something (a process, a relationship, a resource), they'll overvalue it. Acknowledge what exists before proposing a change. Don't position the new as replacing the old; position it as building on the old.

## Emotional Resonance (Pink + Sinek)

- **Attunement first**: Before writing, model how the audience sees the situation. What are their concerns, their constraints, their language? Write in their frame, not yours.
- **Buoyancy in tone**: Maintain genuine conviction without sounding pushy. Interrogative confidence ("Isn't this worth exploring?") often lands better than declarative confidence ("This is the best option").
- **Peak-End Rule**: The most remembered parts will be the emotional peak and the conclusion. Design both deliberately. End with something that resonates, not with logistics.

## For Negotiations Specifically

- **Circle of Safety** (Sinek): Make the other party feel psychologically safe before making requests. Reduce threat signals.
- **Caveat venditor** (Pink): Frame as service, not selling. "Will their situation genuinely improve if they agree?"
- **Context as product** (Sutherland): Where and how you present matters. The same proposal in a formal document vs. a casual email vs. in person shifts perception dramatically.
- **Regret framing** (Pink): For decisions the other party is hesitating on, invoke the boldness regret test — "In five years, will you regret not trying this?"
