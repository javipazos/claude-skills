# Kahneman — Quick Reference for Persuasion

## Loss Aversion (Prospect Theory, 1979; TFS Ch. 26-34)
Losses loom ~2x larger than gains. Frame around what they lose by NOT acting. In negotiations: structure proposals as preventing losses, not achieving gains.

## Anchoring (TFS Ch. 11; Tversky & Kahneman, Science 1974)
First number sets the reference. Present the ambitious option first — everything after feels reasonable. Generate your own estimate before hearing theirs.

## WYSIATI — What You See Is All There Is (TFS Ch. 7)
People build the best story from available info without noticing what's missing. Counter: surface missing information proactively. FAQ sections, "risks and mitigations," transparency.

## Peak-End Rule (TED "The Riddle of Experience vs. Memory", 2010; TFS Ch. 35-38)
Experiences are judged by peak moment + ending, not duration. Engineer the ending of every presentation, meeting, page, conversation.

## Planning Fallacy / Outside View (TFS Ch. 23)
People underestimate time/cost/risk by focusing on the specific case (inside view). Counter: cite comparable cases. "Organizations like ours who did X saw Y."

## Premortem (TFS Part IV)
Before committing: assume it failed. Why? This activates knowledge that optimism suppresses. Include proactively in proposals to build trust.

## Satisficing + Endowment Effect (TFS Ch. 27)
People overvalue what they already have. Acknowledge existing processes/relationships before proposing change. Don't replace; build on.

## Noise (Noise, 2021)
Inconsistency in presentation triggers suspicion. Use consistent structure, formatting, and framing throughout documents and arguments.

## Framing Effects (TFS)
"1 in 1,000 will die" vs. "99.9% survival rate" — identical fact, different emotional response. Choose your frame deliberately.
