# Stoic Philosophy — Reflection Lens

Core teachings from the three principal Stoic authors, grounded in their original texts.

## The Dichotomy of Control
**Source:** Epictetus, *Enchiridion* (The Handbook), Section 1

"Some things are within our power, while others are not. Within our power are opinion, motivation, desire, aversion, and, in a word, whatever is of our own doing. Not within our power are our body, our property, reputation, office, and, in a word, whatever is not of our own doing."

The foundational Stoic principle. Before reacting to any situation, ask: is this within my control or not? If not, redirect energy to what is.

**What's within control:** Your judgments, intentions, desires, aversions, responses, effort, character.
**What's NOT within control:** Other people's opinions, external outcomes, your reputation, the past, the weather, the economy, other people's behavior.

**Application:** When anxious about an outcome, identify what's within your sphere of action and focus exclusively there. Release attachment to what isn't.

## Premeditatio Malorum (Negative Visualization)
**Source:** Seneca, *Letters to Lucilius* (Moral Letters), Letter 91; also Marcus Aurelius, *Meditations*, Book XI

"We should project our thoughts ahead of us at every turn and have in mind every possible eventuality instead of only the usual course of events." — Seneca, Letter 91

Deliberately imagine worst-case scenarios — not to become anxious, but to reduce their emotional power and prepare rational responses. This is the ancient version of Kahneman's premortem.

**Application:** Before any important endeavor, ask: What is the worst that could happen? How would I respond? Could I survive it? This reduces fear and improves preparation.

## Amor Fati (Love of Fate)
**Source:** Marcus Aurelius, *Meditations*, Book V.8; Epictetus, *Discourses*, Book I.12

"Accept the things to which fate binds you, and love the people with whom fate brings you together, and do so with all your heart." — Marcus Aurelius, *Meditations* V.8

Not just acceptance but embrace of everything that happens — including adversity. The obstacle becomes the way. What happens to you is material for growth, not a hindrance to it.

**Application:** When something goes wrong, ask: How can this serve me? What does this make possible that wasn't possible before?

## The Obstacle Is the Way
**Source:** Marcus Aurelius, *Meditations*, Book V.20

"The impediment to action advances action. What stands in the way becomes the way."

Every obstacle contains within it the seed of an equal or greater benefit — not in a naive "everything happens for a reason" sense, but in the practical sense that adversity forces adaptation, creativity, and growth that comfort never does.

**Application:** When blocked, ask: What skill, relationship, or insight is this obstacle forcing me to develop?

## Memento Mori (Remember Death)
**Source:** Marcus Aurelius, *Meditations*, Book II.4; Seneca, *On the Shortness of Life*

"You could leave life right now. Let that determine what you do and say and think." — Marcus Aurelius, *Meditations* II.4

"It is not that we have a short time to live, but that we waste a great deal of it." — Seneca, *On the Shortness of Life*

Awareness of mortality is not morbid — it's clarifying. It cuts through trivial concerns and reveals what actually matters. This complements Pink's regret framework: at the end, what will you wish you had done?

**Application:** Is what I'm worrying about right now something I'd care about on my deathbed? If not, redirect attention to what I would care about.

## The View from Above
**Source:** Marcus Aurelius, *Meditations*, Book IX.30; Book VII.48

"How small a part of the boundless and unfathomable time is assigned to every man! For it is very soon swallowed up in the eternal. And how small a part of the whole substance! And how small a part of the universal soul!" — *Meditations* IX.30

Zoom out. See your situation from the perspective of the cosmos, of centuries, of the vastness of human experience. Most of what feels urgent and enormous is, in the larger picture, a small blip. This is the Stoic antidote to Kahneman's focusing illusion.

**Application:** When emotions run high, mentally zoom out. Will this matter in a year? In ten years? In the story of your life?

## Virtue as the Sole Good
**Source:** Epictetus, *Discourses*, Book I.4; Seneca, *Letters to Lucilius*, Letter 71

The four Stoic virtues — **Wisdom** (knowing what's truly good, bad, and indifferent), **Courage** (acting rightly despite fear), **Justice** (treating others fairly), **Temperance** (self-control and moderation) — are the only true goods. Everything else (wealth, health, reputation) is "preferred indifferent" — nice to have but not necessary for a good life.

"Wealth consists not in having great possessions, but in having few wants." — Epictetus

**Application:** When making a moral decision, ask: Does this choice align with wisdom, courage, justice, and temperance? If it conflicts with any of the four, it's the wrong choice regardless of material benefit.

## On Anger
**Source:** Seneca, *On Anger* (De Ira), Books I-III

"The best remedy for anger is delay." — Seneca, *On Anger* II.29

Anger is a judgment that something unjust has been done to you, combined with a desire for revenge. Seneca's prescription: delay response. The initial flash is involuntary (System 1), but the decision to act on it is within your control (System 2). Give yourself space between stimulus and response.

"How much more grievous are the consequences of anger than the causes of it." — *On Anger* I.1

**Application:** When angry, pause. Ask: Is this truly unjust, or merely unexpected? Is my anger proportional to the actual harm? What would the wise response be if I weren't angry?

## Journal Practice
**Source:** Marcus Aurelius, *Meditations* (the entire work is a journal); Seneca, *Letters to Lucilius*, Letter 83

The *Meditations* were never intended for publication — they are Marcus Aurelius's private journal, written to himself as emperor of Rome. The practice of daily self-examination is itself a core Stoic exercise.

Seneca's daily review (Letter 83): "When the light has been removed and my wife has fallen silent... I examine my entire day and go back over what I've done and said, hiding nothing from myself and passing nothing by."

**Application:** End each day with a brief review. What went well? What didn't? Where did I act according to my values, and where did I fall short? This is the Stoic version of continuous improvement.

## Sympatheia (Interconnection)
**Source:** Marcus Aurelius, *Meditations*, Book VI.38; Book IV.40

"Meditate often on the interconnectedness and mutual interdependence of all things in the universe." — *Meditations* VI.38

We are parts of a whole. What harms one harms all; what benefits one benefits all. This grounds Sinek's concept of service-first leadership in a 2,000-year-old philosophical tradition.

**Application:** Before acting selfishly, consider the ripple effects. How does this action affect the web of relationships I'm part of?

## Key Tensions Between Stoicism and the Modern Authors

- **Stoicism + Kahneman:** Both advocate structural remedies over willpower. The Stoics prescribe daily practices (journal, premeditation, view from above); Kahneman prescribes process design (premortems, reference class forecasting, noise audits). They complement each other.
- **Stoicism + Sutherland:** Tension. Stoics are suspicious of perception games — they seek truth beneath appearances. Sutherland celebrates perception as the only reality that matters. The reconciliation: understand perception (Sutherland) but don't be enslaved by it (Stoics).
- **Stoicism + Pink:** Complementary. The Stoic virtues map to Pink's Purpose. The dichotomy of control maps to Autonomy (focus on what you can control). Memento mori and the regret framework both use mortality as a clarifying lens.
- **Stoicism + Sinek:** Deeply compatible. The Stoic leader serves the group (sympatheia); Sinek's leader eats last. The Stoic plays the infinite game by definition — virtue is the direction, not the destination.
