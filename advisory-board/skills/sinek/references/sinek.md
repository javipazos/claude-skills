# Simon Sinek — Frameworks and Teachings

## The Golden Circle
**Source:** *Start With Why* (2009), TED Talk "How Great Leaders Inspire Action" (2009)

Three concentric circles: WHY (purpose/belief) → HOW (values/process) → WHAT (products/output).

"People don't buy what you do; they buy why you do it."

Most organizations communicate outside-in (WHAT→WHY). Inspired organizations communicate inside-in (WHY→WHAT). The limbic brain — which governs decisions — responds to WHY, not WHAT. Feature lists rarely change behavior; shared belief does.

**Key examples:**
- Apple communicates belief first ("We believe in challenging the status quo"), product second
- Wright Brothers vs. Samuel Langley: the Wright Brothers had no funding but a WHY (powered flight changes the world). Langley had $50K from the War Department but wanted fame. When the Wrights succeeded, Langley quit.
- MLK gave the "I Have a Dream" speech, not the "I Have a Plan" speech

**Law of Diffusion of Innovation** (from the TED talk): Innovators 2.5%, Early Adopters 13.5%, then majorities. Critical threshold: 15-18%. Don't try to convince everyone — focus your WHY on true believers first. They socially proof it for the majority.

## Manipulation vs. Inspiration
**Source:** *Start With Why*, Chapter 2 ("Carrots and Sticks")

Two ways to influence behavior:
- **Manipulations**: price reductions, promotions, fear, peer pressure, novelty. They work but produce transactions, not loyalty. "Manipulations lead to transactions, not loyalty."
- **Inspiration**: resonates with existing beliefs. Works at a premium, tolerates inconvenience, generates advocacy.

**Test:** If your tactic requires giving the customer something new to act, it's manipulation. If it works by resonating with something they already believe, it's inspiration.

## The Celery Test
**Source:** *Start With Why*, Chapter 10

Decision filter: if your WHY is healthy living, you buy celery at the store, not M&Ms — even if advisors recommend both. Your WHY filters decisions automatically.

**Before any strategic decision, ask:** "Is this our celery or our M&Ms?"

## The Circle of Safety
**Source:** *Leaders Eat Last* (2014)

A cultural perimeter inside which people feel psychologically safe. When people feel safe from internal threats (politics, blame, being undermined), they redirect energy toward external threats and opportunities.

"The whole purpose of maintaining the circle of safety is so that we can invest all our time and energy to guard against dangers outside."

When the circle is weak: people hoard information, cover themselves, compete internally. Innovation dies.
When the circle is strong: people share bad news early, take risks, collaborate openly.

**The EDSO Model (Four Leadership Chemicals):**
- **Endorphins**: mask pain (runner's high)
- **Dopamine**: reward for achieving goals — addictive, drives next task
- **Serotonin**: pride, status, being respected by the group — reinforces leaders who serve
- **Oxytocin**: bonding chemical — built through generosity and time together, builds trust slowly

Great leaders produce serotonin and oxytocin. Fear-based managers produce cortisol.

**Key example:** Barry-Wehmiller CEO Bob Chapman during 2008 crisis chose mandatory unpaid furloughs for all levels (including executives) rather than targeted layoffs. Everyone shares pain equally; no one is expendable.

## The WHY Statement
**Source:** *Find Your Why* (2017)

Formula: "To [contribution you make] so that [the impact of that contribution]."

Sinek's own: "To inspire people to do the things that inspire them so that, together, we can change our world."

Must be: one sentence, action-oriented, focused on effect on others, authentic (discovered, not invented).

**Discovery process:** Gather stories of defining moments → identify repeating themes → draft statement.

**Nested WHYs:** Organizations have a trunk WHY, branches (departments), and nests (teams). Misalignment at any level produces friction. Trace team dysfunction upward to find the misaligned WHY.

## The Infinite Game
**Source:** *The Infinite Game* (2019), building on James P. Carse's *Finite and Infinite Games* (1986)

**Finite games:** known players, fixed rules, defined endpoint (chess, football).
**Infinite games:** unknown players, evolving rules, no endpoint. Goal is to keep playing.

Business, politics, education are infinite games. A finite-minded player (beat this competitor) against an infinite-minded player (advance the cause) always loses eventually.

**The Five Practices:**

1. **Advance a Just Cause** — a vision of a future state so appealing people sacrifice for it. Must be: for something (not against), inclusive, service-oriented, resilient, idealistic (a direction, not a destination).

2. **Build Trusting Teams** — Navy SEALs prioritize medium performer/high character over high performer/low character. A toxic high performer poisons the team.

3. **Study Worthy Rivals** — not competitors to beat, but players who reveal your weaknesses. "What do they do better, and what can we learn?" vs. "How do we beat them?"

4. **Existential Flexibility** — capacity to disrupt your own model to advance the Just Cause. Netflix abandoned profitable DVD-by-mail for streaming. "Be stubborn on vision, flexible on how you get there."

5. **Courage to Lead** — maintaining infinite perspective under pressure from quarterly earnings, media narratives about "winning."

**Decision test:** "Are we making this decision to advance our Just Cause, or to beat a specific competitor?"

## Together Is Better
**Source:** *Together Is Better* (2016)

Core principles:
- "A team is not a group of people that work together. A team is a group of people that trust each other."
- "Working hard for something we don't care about is called stress. Working hard for something we love is called passion."
- "Fight against something and we focus on the thing we hate. Fight for something and we focus on the thing we love."

**Leadership test:** Is your energy directed toward an enemy or toward a vision? The former produces anxiety; the latter produces creativity.
